# Trigonometry and the Unit Circle

Trigonometry is the study of the relationships between the side lengths and angles of triangles. While it starts with right-angled triangles, the concept of the "Unit Circle" allows us to apply trigonometry to any angle and to waves.

## SOH CAH TOA: The Basics

For a right-angled triangle with an angle θ:
- **Sine (sin θ)** = Opposite / Hypotenuse
- **Cosine (cos θ)** = Adjacent / Hypotenuse
- **Tangent (tan θ)** = Opposite / Adjacent

## The Unit Circle: Expanding the View

The Unit Circle is a circle with a radius of exactly 1, centred at the origin (0,0). For any point (x,y) on the circle at an angle θ:
- **x = cos θ**
- **y = sin θ**
- **tan θ = y/x**

This means that as you move around the circle, the x and y coordinates naturally create the periodic "waves" of the sine and cosine functions.
- **Radians**: In advanced math, we use radians instead of degrees. A full circle is 2π radians. π radians is 180 degrees.

## Key Identities

Trigonometric identities are equations that are always true.
1. **Pythagorean Identity**: sin² θ + cos² θ = 1. This comes directly from the Pythagorean theorem (x² + y² = r²) applied to the unit circle (r=1).
2. **Tangent Identity**: tan θ = sin θ / cos θ.
3. **Double Angle Formulas**: Used to simplify complex expressions, like sin(2θ) = 2 sin θ cos θ.

## Inverse Functions

If you know the side lengths and want to find the angle, you use inverse functions: **arcsin**, **arccos**, and **arctan**. Note: sin⁻¹(x) is *not* the same as 1/sin(x). The latter is cosecant (csc).

## Common Misconceptions

A major misconception is that sin, cos, and tan are "numbers" you multiply by θ. They are **functions**; they are operations performed *on* θ. Another error is thinking that tan(90°) is just a very big number. It is mathematically undefined because at 90°, cos(90°) is zero, and you cannot divide by zero. Finally, students often get confused between the "Inverse" (arcsin) and the "Reciprocal" (cosecant). They are completely different concepts!

## Analogy: The Ferris Wheel

Imagine a Ferris wheel with a radius of 1 meter.
- **Sine** is your height above the ground as the wheel turns. At the start (0°), you are at height 0. At the top (90°), you are at height 1.
- **Cosine** is your horizontal distance from the centre pole. At the start (0°), you are 1 meter away. At the top (90°), you are directly above the pole (0 meters away).
As the wheel keeps spinning, your height and distance follow a perfect, repeating wave pattern.
