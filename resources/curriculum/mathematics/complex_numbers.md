# Complex Numbers

Complex numbers expand the number system to include the solutions to equations that have no real answers, such as x² = -1. They are essential in electrical engineering, physics, and advanced mathematics.

## The Imaginary Unit (i)

We define the imaginary unit **i** as: **i² = -1** or **i = √-1**.
A complex number is written in the form **a + bi**, where:
- **a** is the **real part**.
- **b** is the **imaginary part**.

If b = 0, the number is purely real. If a = 0, the number is purely imaginary.

## Operations with Complex Numbers

- **Addition/Subtraction**: Just like combining like terms in algebra. Add the real parts and add the imaginary parts.
  (3 + 2i) + (1 + 4i) = (3+1) + (2+4)i = 4 + 6i.
- **Multiplication**: Use FOIL (First, Outside, Inside, Last). Remember that i² = -1.
  (2 + i)(3 - i) = 6 - 2i + 3i - i² = 6 + i - (-1) = 7 + i.
- **Division**: To divide, we multiply the top and bottom by the **complex conjugate** of the denominator. If the denominator is (a + bi), the conjugate is (a - bi). This turns the denominator into a real number.

## The Complex Plane (Argand Diagram)

We can't plot complex numbers on a standard number line. Instead, we use a 2D plane where the x-axis is the **Real axis** and the y-axis is the **Imaginary axis**. The number 3 + 2i would be a point at coordinates (3, 2).

## Polar Form and Euler's Formula

Complex numbers can also be described by their distance from the origin (**r**, the modulus) and the angle they make with the positive real axis (**θ**, the argument).
Using trigonometry: **z = r(cos θ + i sin θ)**.
Leonhard Euler discovered the most beautiful formula in math, linking these to the exponential function:
**e^(iθ) = cos θ + i sin θ**
This leads to the famous **Euler's Identity**: **e^(iπ) + 1 = 0**, which connects five of the most important constants in mathematics.

## Common Misconceptions

The name "imaginary" is the biggest misconception. These numbers are just as "real" and useful as any other number; the name was originally intended as an insult by mathematicians who didn't like them. Another error is thinking that i = √-1 can be used in the same way as positive square roots. For example, √-1 × √-1 is -1, but using the rule √a × √b = √ab would incorrectly give √(-1 × -1) = √1 = 1. This is why we always use the 'i' notation.

## Analogy: The Sideways Step

Imagine you are standing on a number line. You can move forward (positive) or backward (negative). But what if you want to turn left or right? Complex numbers are like a "sideways step" off the line. Multiplying by **i** is equivalent to a 90-degree turn. If you multiply by **i** again, you've turned 180 degrees — which faces you in the negative direction. That's why i × i = -1!
