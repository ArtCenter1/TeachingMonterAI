# Statistics and Probability

Statistics is the science of collecting, analysing, and interpreting data. Probability is the branch of math that deals with the likelihood of events occurring. Together, they allow us to make sense of an uncertain world.

## Probability: The Math of Chance

The probability of an event (P) is always between 0 (impossible) and 1 (certain).
- **Independent Events**: One event doesn't affect the other (e.g., flipping a coin twice). We multiply their probabilities: P(A and B) = P(A) × P(B).
- **Dependent Events**: One event changes the likelihood of the next (e.g., drawing two cards from a deck without putting the first one back).
- **Conditional Probability**: The probability of A occurring *given that* B has already occurred. P(A|B).

## Descriptive Statistics: Summarising Data

- **Mean**: The average (sum divided by count).
- **Median**: The middle value when data is ordered.
- **Mode**: The most frequent value.
- **Range**: The difference between the highest and lowest values.
- **Standard Deviation**: A measure of how spread out the data is. A low standard deviation means most data is close to the mean; a high one means it's widely scattered.

## The Normal Distribution (Bell Curve)

In nature, many things (like human height or test scores) follow a Normal Distribution. It is symmetrical, with most data clustered around the mean.
- **The 68-95-99.7 Rule**: In a perfect normal distribution, 68% of data falls within 1 standard deviation of the mean, 95% within 2, and 99.7% within 3.

## Inferential Statistics: Making Guesses

We often take a **sample** of a large population and use it to make a guess about the whole group.
- **Hypothesis Testing**: A way to check if a result is "statistically significant" or just happened by pure chance. We usually use a "p-value." If p < 0.05, we say the result is significant.

## Common Misconceptions

A huge misconception is the "Gambler's Fallacy" — thinking that if a coin has landed on heads 10 times in a row, it is "due" to land on tails. The coin has no memory; every flip is exactly 50/50. Another error is confusing "Correlation" with "Causation." Just because two things happen at the same time (e.g., ice cream sales and shark attacks both go up in summer) doesn't mean one causes the other (heat causes both). Finally, students often think the "Mean" is always the best average. If a dataset has "outliers" (one extremely large or small number), the Median is usually a much better representation of the data.

## Analogy: The Weather Forecast

When a meteorologist says there is a "70% chance of rain," they aren't saying it *will* rain. They are saying that in the past, under these exact atmospheric conditions, it rained 7 times out of 10. Probability gives you a map of possibilities, but it doesn't tell you exactly what will happen today.
