The study of Mendelian Genetics and Heredity forms a cornerstone of modern biology, providing the fundamental framework for understanding how traits are passed from one generation to the next. At its heart, heredity is simply the transmission of characteristics from parents to their offspring, a phenomenon we observe every day in families, from eye color to hair texture. Genetics, then, is the scientific discipline dedicated to unraveling the mechanisms and patterns of this inheritance. Our foundational understanding largely stems from the meticulous work of Gregor Mendel, an Austrian monk who, in the mid-19th century, conducted groundbreaking experiments with pea plants. His careful observations and quantitative analysis, long before the discovery of DNA, revealed predictable patterns of inheritance, earning him the title "Father of Genetics."

Core to Mendelian genetics is the concept of a gene, which we now know is a specific segment of DNA located on a chromosome, acting as a unit of heredity that codes for a particular characteristic. For each gene, there can be different versions, known as alleles. For instance, a gene for pea plant height might have one allele for tallness and another for shortness. Organisms inherit two alleles for each gene, one from each parent. The interaction between these two alleles determines the observable trait. When these two alleles are identical, the individual is said to be homozygous for that gene (e.g., two tall alleles or two short alleles). If the two alleles are different, the individual is heterozygous (e.g., one tall and one short allele).

Mendel's genius lay in identifying the concepts of dominant and recessive alleles. A dominant allele is one that expresses its trait whenever it is present, effectively masking the presence of a recessive allele. A recessive allele, conversely, only expresses its trait when two copies of it are present, meaning the individual is homozygous recessive. For example, if the allele for tallness (let's denote it with 'T') is dominant over the allele for shortness ('t'), a pea plant with genotype TT or Tt will be tall, while only a plant with genotype tt will be short. This distinction leads to two crucial terms: genotype and phenotype. Genotype refers to the specific genetic makeup of an organism, the actual combination of alleles it possesses (e.g., TT, Tt, tt). Phenotype, on the other hand, describes the observable physical or biochemical characteristics that result from that genotype (e.g., tall, short).
