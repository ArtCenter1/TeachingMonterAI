# Hormones and the Endocrine System

The endocrine system is the body's chemical messaging network, operating through hormones — signalling molecules secreted directly into the bloodstream that travel to distant target organs and trigger specific physiological responses. Unlike the nervous system, which operates in milliseconds via electrical impulses, the endocrine system acts over seconds to hours, producing effects that are often longer-lasting, such as growth, metabolism, and reproductive cycles.

## Core Concept: What Makes a Hormone

A hormone is defined not by its chemical structure but by its function: it is released into the blood by a gland or tissue and acts on a distant target cell that expresses a specific receptor protein for that hormone. This lock-and-key specificity means that adrenaline flooding the bloodstream will trigger the heart (which has beta-adrenergic receptors) to beat faster, but will not stimulate a kidney tubule cell in the same way. Hormones are fundamentally context-dependent: their effect depends entirely on which receptor the target cell displays.

Chemically, hormones fall into two broad categories: steroid hormones (derived from cholesterol, e.g. cortisol, oestrogen, testosterone) and non-steroid hormones (proteins, peptides, or amines, e.g. insulin, adrenaline, TSH). This distinction is critical for understanding how they work. Steroid hormones are lipid-soluble; they diffuse directly through the plasma membrane and bind to intracellular receptors, forming a hormone-receptor complex that acts as a transcription factor — directly altering which genes are expressed. Non-steroid hormones cannot cross the membrane; they bind to surface receptors and trigger an intracellular signalling cascade (often involving cyclic AMP as a second messenger) that ultimately changes the cell's behaviour without altering gene expression.

## Key Glands and Their Hormones

The hypothalamus-pituitary axis is the master control centre. The hypothalamus, embedded in the brain, releases regulatory hormones (e.g. CRH, TRH, GnRH) into a short portal blood vessel connecting it to the anterior pituitary gland. The anterior pituitary responds by releasing tropic hormones (ACTH, TSH, FSH, LH, GH, prolactin) that in turn stimulate peripheral glands. This creates elegant three-tier cascades.

The thyroid gland, responding to TSH, releases thyroxine (T4) and triiodothyronine (T3), which accelerate metabolic rate in virtually every cell. Hypothyroidism (insufficient T3/T4) causes fatigue, weight gain, and cold intolerance; hyperthyroidism causes the opposite. The feedback loop is classic: rising T3/T4 in the blood feeds back to inhibit both the hypothalamus and the pituitary, reducing TRH and TSH release respectively.

The adrenal glands sit atop each kidney and have two distinct regions. The adrenal cortex (outer) releases steroid hormones: cortisol (the stress hormone — raises blood glucose, suppresses immunity, mobilises fat) and aldosterone (regulates sodium and water reabsorption in the kidney). The adrenal medulla (inner) is functionally part of the nervous system; it releases adrenaline (epinephrine) and noradrenaline directly into the blood as part of the fight-or-flight response, rapidly increasing heart rate, dilating bronchioles, and shunting blood to muscles.

The pancreas is both an exocrine gland (secreting digestive enzymes into the gut) and an endocrine gland. Its islets of Langerhans contain alpha cells (glucagon) and beta cells (insulin). When blood glucose rises after a meal, beta cells release insulin, which directs liver, muscle, and fat cells to take up and store glucose (as glycogen or fat). When blood glucose falls during fasting or exercise, alpha cells release glucagon, which signals the liver to break down glycogen and release glucose. This antagonistic pairing is a superb example of homeostatic control.

## Common Misconceptions

Students frequently confuse "hormone" with "enzyme" — both are biological molecules that act on specific targets, but enzymes are catalysts that speed up reactions (often inside the same cell that made them), while hormones are long-distance messengers. Another common error is thinking that all hormones stimulate their targets. Inhibitory hormones are equally important: somatostatin from the pancreas inhibits both insulin and glucagon secretion; growth-hormone-inhibiting hormone from the hypothalamus prevents excess GH release. A third misconception is that hormones can only affect one thing — in reality, the same hormone (e.g. cortisol) can have many effects on many organs simultaneously, because many different cell types express cortisol receptors.

## Negative Feedback: The Thermostat Analogy

The dominant regulatory mechanism in the endocrine system is negative feedback, exactly like a household thermostat. Set the thermostat to 20°C. If the room cools to 18°C, the heater fires (analogous to the pituitary releasing TSH when blood T3/T4 falls). When the room reaches 20°C, the thermostat detects this and shuts the heater off (analogous to rising T3/T4 inhibiting TRH and TSH production). The result is stable body temperature (or stable hormone levels) maintained around a set point.

Positive feedback loops also exist but are less common because they are inherently destabilising and must be self-terminating. The LH surge during the menstrual cycle is a classic example: rising oestrogen from developing follicles initially causes positive feedback on the pituitary, triggering a massive LH surge that induces ovulation — after which the follicle ruptures and oestrogen falls, terminating the loop.

## Clinical Connections

Diabetes mellitus type 1 results from autoimmune destruction of pancreatic beta cells, eliminating insulin production. Without insulin, cells cannot take up glucose; blood glucose rises (hyperglycaemia), while cells starve — a metabolic paradox. Type 2 diabetes involves insulin resistance: beta cells still produce insulin, but target cells have downregulated their receptors (often due to chronic overstimulation from a high-carbohydrate diet).

Cushing's syndrome arises from chronically elevated cortisol (from a cortisol-secreting tumour or long-term steroid medication). Its hallmark features — central obesity (fat redistribution), immunosuppression, muscle wasting, thinning skin, and hypertension — map directly onto cortisol's known actions in those tissues.
