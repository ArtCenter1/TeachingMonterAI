# The Molecular Basis of Inheritance: DNA Structure

## 1. Introduction
DNA (Deoxyribonucleic acid) is the molecule that carries genetic information for the development and functioning of an organism. Its structure was famously determined by James Watson and Francis Crick in 1953, building on the critical work of Rosalind Franklin (X-ray diffraction) and Erwin Chargaff.

## 2. The Double Helix
DNA consists of two long chains of nucleotides twisted into a double helix.

### Component Parts:
Each nucleotide is made of three components:
1.  **Deoxyribose Sugar:** A 5-carbon sugar.
2.  **Phosphate Group:** Forms the "backbone" with the sugar.
3.  **Nitrogenous Base:** The "rungs" of the ladder.

## 3. Nitrogenous Bases and Base Pairing
There are four types of nitrogenous bases in DNA:
*   **Purines (Double-ring):** Adenine (A) and Guanine (G).
*   **Pyrimidines (Single-ring):** Thymine (T) and Cytosine (C).

### Chargaff's Rules:
Base pairing is highly specific:
*   **Adenine (A)** always pairs with **Thymine (T)** (2 hydrogen bonds).
*   **Guanine (G)** always pairs with **Cytosine (C)** (3 hydrogen bonds).

## 4. Antiparallel Strands
The two strands of DNA run in opposite directions.
*   One strand runs in the **5' to 3'** direction.
*   The other runs in the **3' to 5'** direction.
This orientation is crucial for the mechanism of DNA replication and transcription.

## 5. DNA Packaging
DNA is a very long molecule. To fit inside the microscopic nucleus of a cell, it is tightly coiled:
*   DNA wraps around proteins called **Histones**.
*   This structure is called a **Nucleosome**.
*   Nucleosomes coil further into **Chromatin**, which condenses into **Chromosomes** during cell division.

## 6. Pedagogical Analogies
*   **The Twisted Ladder:** The sugar-phosphate backbones are the sides of the ladder, and the base pairs are the rungs.
*   **The Library Metaphor:** DNA is the "master blueprint" in the library (nucleus). You don't take it out; you make a photocopy (mRNA) to take to the construction site (ribosome).

## 7. Common Student Misconceptions
*   **Misconception:** All DNA is "genes."
*   **Correction:** Only a small percentage of human DNA actually codes for proteins (the genes). Much of it is regulatory or non-coding.
*   **Misconception:** DNA is static.
*   **Correction:** DNA is dynamic; it can mutate, be repaired, and its expression is constantly regulated by the environment (epigenetics).
