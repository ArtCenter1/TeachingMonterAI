# The Nervous System and Neurons

The nervous system is the body's high-speed communication network, using electrical and chemical signals to process information, coordinate actions, and maintain homeostasis. It is functionally divided into the Central Nervous System (CNS) — the brain and spinal cord — and the Peripheral Nervous System (PNS), which connects the CNS to the rest of the body.

## The Building Blocks: Neurons and Glia

Neurons are the fundamental units of the nervous system, specialised to generate and conduct electrical impulses. While they vary in shape, most share a common structure: a cell body (soma) containing the nucleus; dendrites that receive incoming signals; an axon that carries the signal away; and axon terminals that transmit the signal to the next cell.

Supporting the neurons are glial cells (glia). In the PNS, Schwann cells wrap around axons to form a myelin sheath — a fatty insulating layer. In the CNS, oligodendrocytes perform the same role. This insulation is interrupted by small gaps called nodes of Ranvier, which are essential for fast signal conduction. Glia also provide structural support, nutrients, and waste removal for neurons.

## The Action Potential: How Neurons "Fire"

Communication within a single neuron is electrical. At rest, a neuron maintains a negative electrical charge inside compared to the outside (the resting potential, roughly -70mV). This is maintained by the sodium-potassium pump, which moves 3 Na+ ions out for every 2 K+ ions in, and by "leak" channels that allow K+ to move out more easily than Na+ can move in.

When a neuron is stimulated at the dendrites, gated ion channels open. If the stimulus is strong enough to reach a specific "threshold" (around -55mV), an action potential is triggered. This is an "all-or-nothing" event:
1. **Depolarisation**: Voltage-gated sodium channels burst open, and Na+ floods into the cell, making the inside positive (+30mV).
2. **Repolarisation**: Sodium channels close and voltage-gated potassium channels open, allowing K+ to rush out, making the inside negative again.
3. **Hyperpolarisation**: Potassium channels are slow to close, causing the potential to drop slightly below the resting level before returning to normal.

In myelinated axons, the signal "jumps" from one node of Ranvier to the next in a process called saltatory conduction. This is much faster and uses less energy than continuous conduction in unmyelinated axons.

## The Synapse: Communication Between Cells

Communication between neurons (or between a neuron and a muscle) happens at the synapse — a small gap where the axon terminal of the presynaptic neuron meets the dendrite or cell body of the postsynaptic neuron.
1. When an action potential reaches the terminal, voltage-gated calcium channels open.
2. Ca2+ entry triggers vesicles to fuse with the membrane and release neurotransmitters (e.g., acetylcholine, dopamine, serotonin) into the synaptic cleft.
3. These chemicals diffuse across and bind to receptors on the postsynaptic membrane.
4. This binding opens ion channels, either exciting the next cell (making it more likely to fire) or inhibiting it.

## Common Misconceptions

A major misconception is that nerves and neurons are the same. A neuron is a single cell, whereas a nerve is a bundle of hundreds or thousands of axons wrapped in connective tissue, much like a multi-strand electrical cable. Another error is thinking that electrical signals "flow" through neurons like electricity through a copper wire; in reality, it is a wave of ions moving across the membrane, which is why it is much slower than the speed of light. Finally, students often think the brain is "hard-wired." In fact, synapses are constantly being formed, strengthened, or pruned based on experience — a property called neuroplasticity.

## Analogy: The Telephone Exchange

Imagine an old-fashioned telephone network. The neurons are the individual wires, and the brain is the central exchange. The action potential is the electrical signal travelling down a wire. The synapse is the operator at the exchange who takes the incoming call and physically plugs a cord into a new socket to send the message to the right destination. Learning a new skill is like the exchange building more efficient routes for certain calls.
