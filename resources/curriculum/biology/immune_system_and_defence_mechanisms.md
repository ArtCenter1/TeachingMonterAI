# The Immune System and Defence Mechanisms

The immune system is the body's multi-layered defence force against pathogens — bacteria, viruses, fungi, parasites — and against abnormal cells such as cancers. It is divided into two broad but interconnected branches: the innate immune system (fast, non-specific, present from birth) and the adaptive immune system (slower to activate, exquisitely specific, and capable of immunological memory).

## First Line: Physical and Chemical Barriers

Before any immune cell is recruited, the body presents formidable structural barriers. The intact skin is impermeable to most pathogens; its slightly acidic pH (4.5–5.5) and resident microbiome create an inhospitable environment. Mucous membranes line all body openings (respiratory tract, GI tract, urogenital tract); the sticky mucus traps particles and the cilia of the respiratory epithelium beat them upward toward the pharynx (the "mucociliary escalator"). Chemical defences reinforce this: lysozyme in tears and saliva breaks down bacterial cell walls; stomach acid (pH 1.5–3.5) destroys most ingested pathogens; and defensin peptides in the skin and gut pores directly rupture bacterial membranes.

## Second Line: Innate Immunity

When pathogens breach the barriers, innate immunity responds within minutes to hours. Innate immune cells (neutrophils, macrophages, natural killer cells, dendritic cells) recognise broad molecular patterns common to microbes — called pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) — via pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) such as Toll-like receptors. This is non-specific: a macrophage responds the same way to a Gram-positive bacterium, a Gram-negative bacterium, or a fungal particle.

Macrophages and dendritic cells in infected tissue release chemical signals called cytokines (including interleukins and interferons) that orchestrate the inflammatory response: local blood vessels dilate (causing redness and heat), become more permeable (causing swelling), and express adhesion molecules that recruit neutrophils from the bloodstream. Neutrophils are the "first responders" — short-lived but highly effective at engulfing and destroying bacteria by phagocytosis. Natural killer cells patrol for cells that have lost their "self" identity markers (MHC class I), destroying virally infected or cancerous cells directly.

The complement system — a cascade of plasma proteins — is activated by bacterial surfaces, antibodies, or spontaneously. Complement produces membrane attack complexes (MACs) that punch holes in bacterial membranes, and fragments (C3b) that opsonise (coat) bacteria, marking them for enhanced phagocytosis.

## Third Line: Adaptive Immunity

The adaptive immune system is activated over days but produces a targeted, memory-forming response. It has two arms.

Humoral immunity is mediated by B lymphocytes (B cells), which mature in bone marrow. Each B cell displays a unique B cell receptor (BCR) that recognises one specific antigen (the foreign molecule). When activated (usually requiring helper T cell signals), B cells proliferate and differentiate into plasma cells, which mass-produce antibodies — Y-shaped glycoproteins with identical specificity to the BCR. Antibodies function by neutralisation (blocking pathogen adhesion), opsonisation (marking for phagocytosis), complement activation, and agglutination (clumping pathogens).

Cell-mediated immunity is executed by T lymphocytes (T cells), matured in the thymus. Cytotoxic T cells (CD8+) recognise infected cells presenting foreign peptides on MHC class I molecules; they trigger apoptosis (programmed cell death) in those cells. Helper T cells (CD4+) orchestrate the response by releasing cytokines that activate both B cells and cytotoxic T cells.

Crucially, after the primary response, a subset of both B and T cells become memory cells — long-lived cells that persist for decades. If the same antigen is encountered again, these memory cells mount a secondary immune response that is faster, stronger, and more effective than the primary response. This is the immunological basis of vaccination.

## Vaccination: Training Without Infection

Vaccines introduce an antigen (attenuated pathogen, inactivated toxin, protein subunit, or mRNA encoding an antigen) in a form that cannot cause disease, but is sufficient to prime the adaptive immune system and generate memory cells. When the vaccinated individual encounters the real pathogen, memory cells enable a rapid response that clears the infection before symptoms develop, or dramatically reduces severity.

## Common Misconceptions

Students often think the immune response is always beneficial. In reality, autoimmune diseases arise when the immune system fails to distinguish self from non-self: in type 1 diabetes, cytotoxic T cells destroy the body's own beta cells; in rheumatoid arthritis, antibodies attack joint cartilage. Allergies are an adaptive immune response (IgE antibodies) against harmless antigens like pollen; the inflammatory response that results is the source of symptoms. A second misconception is that antibiotics "boost" the immune system — they are chemical agents that specifically target bacterial cell structures (cell wall synthesis, ribosomes) with no direct immunostimulatory effect.

## Analogy: The Army with a Memory

The innate immune system is like a standing army stationed at the border — it responds immediately to any invasion with brute force. The adaptive immune system is like specialised intelligence units that identify the specific enemy, neutralise them precisely, and file a detailed intelligence report (memory cells) so the next encounter is defeated even faster. Vaccines are equivalent to military training exercises using a mock enemy.
