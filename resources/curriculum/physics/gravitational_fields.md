# Gravitational Fields

Gravity is a universal force of attraction that exists between any two objects with mass. A gravitational field is a region of space in which an object experiences a force due to its mass.

## Newton's Law of Universal Gravitation

Isaac Newton proposed that the force of gravity (F) between two masses (m1 and m2) is directly proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance (r) between their centres.
F = G × (m1 × m2) / r²
where G is the Universal Gravitational Constant (6.67 × 10^-11 N m²/kg²).

This "inverse-square law" means that if you double the distance between two planets, the gravitational pull between them becomes four times weaker (2² = 4).

## Gravitational Field Strength (g)

Field strength is the force per unit mass at a point in the field. On Earth's surface, this is approximately 9.81 N/kg (often rounded to 10 for simplicity). It is calculated as:
g = F / m = G × M / r²
where M is the mass of the planet. Notice that the field strength depends on the mass of the planet and the distance from its centre, but *not* on the mass of the object experiencing the force. This is why a hammer and a feather fall at the same rate in a vacuum.

## Potential and Energy

**Gravitational Potential (V)** is the work done per unit mass to move an object from infinity to a point in the field. It is always negative because gravity is an attractive force.
**Gravitational Potential Energy (GPE)** is the energy an object has due to its position in a field. Near the Earth's surface, we use the simple formula: GPE = mgh. However, for large distances (like satellites in orbit), we must use: GPE = -G × M × m / r.

## Orbital Motion and Weightlessness

For an object to stay in a circular orbit, the gravitational force must provide the necessary centripetal force. This leads to a specific orbital velocity for any given altitude.
A common point of confusion is "weightlessness" in the International Space Station. The astronauts aren't weightless because there's no gravity (Earth's gravity is still about 90% as strong up there). They feel weightless because they are in a state of constant **free-fall**. Both the astronauts and the station are falling toward Earth at the same rate, but they have enough horizontal velocity that they keep "missing" the ground and curving around the planet.

## Common Misconceptions

The most persistent misconception is that there is no gravity in space. Gravity has an infinite range; it just gets weaker with distance. Another error is confusing "mass" (the amount of matter, measured in kg) with "weight" (the force of gravity on that matter, measured in Newtons). Your mass stays the same on the Moon, but your weight changes because the Moon's gravitational field strength is much lower.

## Analogy: The Rubber Sheet

Imagine a large, stretched rubber sheet. If you place a heavy bowling ball in the centre, it creates a "well" or dip in the sheet. This dip represents the gravitational field. If you roll a marble across the sheet, it will be pulled toward the bowling ball. If you roll the marble fast enough, it might circle around the dip — this is like an orbit. This analogy, originally from Einstein's General Relativity, helps visualise how mass "curves" the space around it.
