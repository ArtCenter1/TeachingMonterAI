# Circuits and Electricity

Electricity is the flow of electric charge, usually in the form of electrons moving through a conductor. An electric circuit is a closed path through which this charge can flow. Understanding circuits requires mastering three fundamental quantities: current, voltage, and resistance.

## The Three Pillars: V, I, and R

1. **Current (I)**: Measured in Amperes (A), current is the rate of flow of charge. One Ampere is one Coulomb of charge passing a point per second. In a circuit, we usually talk about "conventional current," which flows from positive to negative, even though the actual electrons flow in the opposite direction.
2. **Voltage (V)**: Also called Potential Difference and measured in Volts (V), voltage is the "push" that makes the charge move. It is the work done per unit charge. A 12V battery gives 12 Joules of energy to every Coulomb of charge.
3. **Resistance (R)**: Measured in Ohms (Ω), resistance is the opposition to the flow of charge. Components like resistors, bulbs, and motors all provide resistance.

These are linked by **Ohm's Law**: V = I × R. This tells us that for a constant resistance, doubling the voltage will double the current.

## Series and Parallel Circuits

The way components are connected changes how current and voltage behave.

**Series Circuits**: Components are connected in a single loop.
- The current is the same everywhere in the circuit.
- The total voltage of the source is shared among the components.
- Total resistance is the sum of all individual resistances: R_total = R1 + R2 + ...
- If one component breaks, the whole circuit stops working.

**Parallel Circuits**: Components are connected in multiple branches.
- The voltage is the same across each branch.
- The total current from the source is the sum of the currents in the individual branches.
- Total resistance decreases as more branches are added: 1/R_total = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + ...
- If one branch breaks, the others continue to function. This is why houses are wired in parallel.

## Power and Energy

Electrical power (P) is the rate at which electrical energy is converted into another form (like heat or light). It is measured in Watts (W) and calculated as: P = I × V.
Since V = IR, we can also write: P = I²R or P = V²/R.
Total energy used (E) is power multiplied by time: E = P × t.

## Common Misconceptions

A massive misconception is that a battery "creates" electrons. It doesn't; the wire is already full of electrons. The battery just provides the energy to make them move. Another error is thinking that current is "used up" as it goes around a circuit. It isn't; the same number of electrons that leave the battery must return to it. It's the *energy* that is transferred to the components. Finally, students often think voltage is "inside" the wires. Voltage is a difference between two points, not a substance flowing through the wire.

## Analogy: The Water Pump System

Imagine a closed loop of water pipes.
- The **Current** is the flow rate of the water (litres per second).
- The **Voltage** is the water pressure provided by a pump.
- The **Resistance** is a narrow section of pipe or a filter that makes it harder for water to pass.
- The **Wires** are the pipes themselves.
If you increase the pump pressure (voltage), the water flows faster (current). If you add more filters (resistance), the flow slows down.
