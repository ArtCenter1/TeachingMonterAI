# Optics and Light

Optics is the branch of physics that studies the behaviour and properties of light. Light is an electromagnetic wave, but it often behaves like a stream of particles called photons. In basic optics, we often use the "ray model," where we represent light as straight lines to show how it interacts with surfaces.

## Reflection and Refraction

When light hits a boundary between two different materials, two main things can happen:

1. **Reflection**: Light bounces off the surface. The **Law of Reflection** states that the angle of incidence is always equal to the angle of reflection (measured from the "normal" — a line perpendicular to the surface). Smooth surfaces like mirrors create "specular" reflection (clear images), while rough surfaces create "diffuse" reflection.
2. **Refraction**: Light passes into the new material and changes speed, which causes it to bend. If light enters a denser material (like from air into glass), it slows down and bends *toward* the normal. The **Refractive Index (n)** of a material is the ratio of the speed of light in a vacuum to the speed of light in that material. Snell's Law (n1 sin θ1 = n2 sin θ2) describes this mathematically.

## Total Internal Reflection (TIR)

When light travels from a denser material to a less dense one (e.g., from water to air), it bends away from the normal. If the angle of incidence is increased enough, the refracted ray eventually travels along the boundary. This is called the **Critical Angle**. Any angle larger than the critical angle results in Total Internal Reflection, where no light escapes and all of it is reflected back inside. This is the principle behind fibre optic cables, which carry internet data as pulses of light over vast distances.

## Lenses and Image Formation

Lenses use refraction to converge or diverge light.
- **Convex (Converging) Lenses**: Thicker in the middle. They bring parallel light rays together at a "focal point." They are used to treat far-sightedness (hyperopia) and in magnifying glasses.
- **Concave (Diverging) Lenses**: Thinner in the middle. They spread light rays apart. They are used to treat near-sightedness (myopia).

## Dispersion and Colour

White light is actually a mixture of all the colours of the rainbow. When white light passes through a prism, different colours refract by different amounts because they have different wavelengths (violet bends the most, red the least). This separation of light into its constituent colours is called **Dispersion**.

## Common Misconceptions

A major misconception is that we see objects because light "comes out of our eyes." We see because light reflects off objects and enters our eyes. Another error is thinking that light always travels in straight lines. While it does in a uniform medium, it bends during refraction and even "curves" around edges in a process called diffraction. Finally, students often think a magnifying glass "creates" more light. It doesn't; it just redirects the light that's already there to fill a larger part of your retina.

## Analogy: The Car on the Sand

Imagine a toy car driving from a smooth pavement onto a patch of sand at an angle. As the first front wheel hits the sand, it slows down while the other front wheel is still on the pavement moving fast. This causes the car to pivot and change direction. This is exactly why light bends during refraction — one side of the wavefront slows down before the other.
