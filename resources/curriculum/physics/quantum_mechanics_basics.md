# Quantum Mechanics Basics

Quantum mechanics is the branch of physics that describes the behaviour of matter and energy at the scale of atoms and subatomic particles. At this level, the "common sense" rules of classical physics (like Newton's Laws) no longer apply, and the universe becomes strange and probabilistic.

## Wave-Particle Duality

One of the most shocking discoveries of quantum mechanics is that everything has both wave-like and particle-like properties.
- **Light**, which we usually think of as a wave, acts as a stream of discrete packets of energy called **Photons** (demonstrated by the Photoelectric Effect).
- **Electrons**, which we usually think of as solid particles, can create interference patterns like waves (demonstrated by the Double Slit Experiment).

This means that an electron isn't a tiny ball orbiting the nucleus like a planet. Instead, it's a "wave of probability" that exists in a fuzzy cloud called an **Orbital**.

## Quantization: Energy in Steps

The word "quantum" means a discrete amount. In the classical world, energy can be any value (like a ramp). In the quantum world, energy is "quantized" (like a staircase). Electrons in an atom can only exist at specific energy levels. To move from a lower level to a higher one, an electron must absorb a specific, exact amount of energy. When it falls back down, it releases that energy as a photon of a specific colour.

## The Uncertainty Principle

Proposed by Werner Heisenberg, this principle states that it is physically impossible to know both the exact position and the exact momentum (speed and direction) of a particle at the same time. The more precisely you measure one, the less precisely you can know the other. This isn't because our instruments are bad; it's a fundamental property of the universe. At the quantum level, the act of observing something changes it.

## Superposition and Entanglement

**Superposition**: A quantum system can exist in multiple states at once until it is measured. This is famously illustrated by "Schrödinger’s Cat," a thought experiment where a cat in a box is both alive and dead until someone looks inside.

**Entanglement**: Two particles can become linked such that the state of one instantly determines the state of the other, no matter how far apart they are. Einstein called this "spooky action at a distance."

## Common Misconceptions

A huge misconception is that "observation" requires a conscious human. In quantum mechanics, an "observation" is any interaction with the environment, such as a photon hitting a particle. Another error is thinking that quantum effects only happen to tiny things. While they are most obvious at the atomic scale, they are the foundation for modern technology like transistors (which run your computer), lasers, and MRI machines. Finally, students often think superposition means the particle is "moving very fast between states." It isn't; it is literally in all states simultaneously.

## Analogy: The Spinning Coin

Imagine a coin spinning on a table. While it's spinning, it's not "heads" and it's not "tails." It's in a **superposition** of both. Only when you slap your hand down on it (make an **observation**) does it "collapse" into one definite state. Before you measured it, it was a "wave" of head-tail probability.
